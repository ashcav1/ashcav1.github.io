01 // RESEARCH

Three ways to interrogate a rubble pile

Most near-Earth asteroids aren't solid rocks — they're flying piles of gravel, boulders, and dust held together by almost nothing. My research spans their deep interiors, their surfaces, and the binary systems they form.

⬡
Under review · Icarus

Peering inside Bennu

OSIRIS-REx delivered the first measured gravity field of a sub-kilometer asteroid. I build heterogeneous density models of (101955) Bennu — superimposed polyhedral components layered onto the shape model — and estimate their densities with an iterative, constrained, covariance-weighted least-squares filter that enforces mass conservation while reproducing the measured gravity coefficients.

The picture that emerges: an under-dense inner core and equatorial torus, balanced by an over-dense subsurface layer — a physically motivated blueprint for how a rubble pile is actually put together.

Active research

Spacecraft meets regolith

Rubble-pile surfaces have surprised every mission that touched one — Bennu's surface swallowed the OSIRIS-REx sampling head tens of centimeters deep. Using LMGC90's non-smooth Contact Dynamics with angular, polyhedral particles, I simulate how landers and instruments mechanically couple to loosely bound, low-gravity regolith — no artificial spring-and-dashpot parameters required.

Touchdown signatures turn out to be diagnostic: penetration depth, contact duration, and rebound encode the cohesion, packing, and friction of the surface they hit.

Building toward

Binary asteroid environments

With GUBAS I model the full two-body gravitational environment of binary systems like Didymos–Dimorphos: the mutual potential, surface gravity and slope, libration, and orbital evolution of two irregular bodies.

The goal is a coupled LMGC90–GUBAS framework in which every simulated patch of surface sits in its true gravity and slope field — making landing, sampling, and hopping across the surface of a binary's secondary genuinely predictable, before a mission has to try it.
